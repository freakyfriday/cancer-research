# Vitamin C — Anti-Cancer Mechanisms & Therapeutic Potential

> **Focus:** How high-dose Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) selectively kills cancer cells, modulates the immune system, enhances conventional therapy, and the clinical evidence supporting its use in oncology.

---

## 1. The Core Mechanism — Pro-Oxidant Hydrogen Peroxide Generation

At low, dietary concentrations Vitamin C acts as an **antioxidant**. However, at **pharmacological concentrations** — achievable only through intravenous (IV) administration — it flips to a **pro-oxidant**, selectively generating lethal doses of hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) in and around cancer cells.

### 1.1 How H₂O₂ Is Generated

1. Ascorbate donates electrons to **redox-active transition metal ions** (Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺ → Cu⁺) in the extracellular fluid
2. These reduced metal ions react with dissolved oxygen
3. This produces **superoxide radicals** which rapidly dismutate into **hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)**
4. H₂O₂ accumulates in the tumour microenvironment at cytotoxic concentrations

### 1.2 Why Cancer Cells Die But Normal Cells Survive

| Factor | Normal Cells | Cancer Cells |
|---|---|---|
| **Catalase levels** | High — efficiently neutralise H₂O₂ | Low / impaired — cannot detoxify the H₂O₂ surge |
| **Basal oxidative stress** | Low | Already elevated — additional H₂O₂ pushes past the lethal threshold |
| **Intracellular iron/copper** | Normal | Often elevated — amplifies the pro-oxidant reaction locally |
| **Antioxidant reserves** | Robust (glutathione peroxidase, SOD) | Depleted or overwhelmed |

> **Key insight:** High-dose IV Vitamin C exploits a fundamental metabolic vulnerability of cancer: their impaired antioxidant defenses and elevated oxidative stress. It acts as a targeted pro-drug — harmless to normal tissue, lethal to malignant cells.

## 2. Epigenetic Reprogramming — TET Enzyme Activation

Vitamin C is a critical **cofactor for TET (Ten-Eleven Translocation) enzymes**, which catalyse DNA demethylation — a process that reactivates silenced tumour suppressor genes.

### 2.1 The TET2 Pathway

1. **TET2** hydroxylates 5-methylcytosine (5mC) → 5-hydroxymethylcytosine (5hmC) → eventual demethylation
2. This turns **ON** genes responsible for cell differentiation and programmed cell death
3. Cancer cells often silence TET2 through mutations — found in ~10% of AML, ~30% of myelodysplastic syndrome, and ~50% of chronic myelomonocytic leukaemia
4. High-dose Vitamin C **restores TET2 activity**, even in cells with only one functional copy of the gene

### 2.2 Consequences of TET Reactivation

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Forced maturation** | Leukaemic stem cells are pushed to differentiate into normal, short-lived blood cells |
| **Self-renewal block** | Cancer stem cell programmes (NANOG, SOX2, OCT4) are suppressed |
| **Tumour suppressor reactivation** | Hypermethylated promoters are demethylated, restoring gene expression |
| **Enhanced chemo-sensitivity** | Epigenetic normalisation makes cancer cells more responsive to drugs like PARP inhibitors and hypomethylating agents (e.g., decitabine, azacitidine) |

> **Key insight:** Vitamin C doesn't just kill cancer cells — it can reprogram them epigenetically, restoring the normal genetic brakes on uncontrolled growth. This is particularly powerful in blood cancers with TET2 mutations.

## 3. HIF-1α Degradation — Cutting Off Tumour Oxygen Adaptation

Tumours thrive in low-oxygen (hypoxic) environments by upregulating **Hypoxia-Inducible Factor 1-alpha (HIF-1α)**, a master transcription factor that drives:

- **Angiogenesis** (new blood vessel formation to feed the tumour)
- **Glycolytic metabolism** (switching to fermentation even when oxygen is available — the Warburg effect)
- **Immune evasion** and treatment resistance
- **Metastasis** gene activation

### How Vitamin C Blocks HIF-1α

Vitamin C is a cofactor for **prolyl hydroxylases (PHDs)** — the enzymes that tag HIF-1α for destruction by the proteasome.

```
Vitamin C → activates PHD enzymes
    │
    ▼
PHDs hydroxylate HIF-1α
    │
    ▼
Hydroxylated HIF-1α is recognised by VHL (von Hippel-Lindau protein)
    │
    ▼
VHL tags HIF-1α with ubiquitin → PROTEASOMAL DEGRADATION
    │
    ▼
Tumour loses ability to adapt to hypoxia, switch metabolism, and grow new blood vessels
```

> **Key insight:** By degrading HIF-1α, Vitamin C attacks the tumour's ability to create its own blood supply, adapt its metabolism, and spread — hitting multiple hallmarks of cancer simultaneously.

## 4. Metabolic Disruption — The Cancer Energy Crisis

High-dose Vitamin C attacks cancer cell metabolism through multiple converging pathways:

### 4.1 NAD⁺ Depletion via PARP Activation

1. H₂O₂ generated by pharmacological ascorbate causes **DNA damage** in cancer cells
2. DNA damage activates **PARP (Poly ADP-Ribose Polymerase)** — a DNA repair enzyme
3. PARP consumes **NAD⁺** as its substrate
4. NAD⁺ depletion halts **glycolysis** (specifically inhibiting GAPDH — glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase)
5. Cancer cells, which are heavily dependent on glycolysis (Warburg effect), experience an **ATP energy crisis** and die

### 4.2 GLUT1 Trojan Horse

- Many cancer cells overexpress **GLUT1** (glucose transporter 1) to fuel their high glycolytic rate
- **Dehydroascorbic acid (DHA)** — the oxidised form of Vitamin C — structurally resembles glucose
- DHA enters cancer cells via GLUT1, competing with glucose uptake
- Inside the cell, DHA is reduced back to ascorbate, consuming glutathione and further depleting antioxidant reserves
- This creates a **double hit**: reduced glucose intake + depleted antioxidant defence

### 4.3 Selective Vulnerability — KRAS & BRAF Mutant Cancers

Colorectal cancer cells harbouring **KRAS or BRAF activating mutations** are particularly vulnerable to Vitamin C because:

- These mutations upregulate GLUT1 expression (more DHA uptake)
- They increase basal ROS levels (less capacity to handle additional oxidative stress)
- They have increased dependence on glycolysis (more vulnerable to NAD⁺ depletion)

> **Key insight:** Vitamin C creates a metabolic trap — cancer cells' own greed for glucose (via GLUT1) becomes the mechanism of their destruction.

## 5. Collagen Synthesis & Anti-Metastatic Barrier (Pauling-Rath Hypothesis)

### 5.1 Strengthening the Extracellular Matrix

Vitamin C is an essential cofactor for **prolyl hydroxylase** and **lysyl hydroxylase** — enzymes required for proper collagen biosynthesis:

1. These enzymes hydroxylate proline and lysine residues in procollagen
2. Hydroxylation enables the stable **collagen triple-helix** conformation
3. This produces strong, dense collagen fibres in the extracellular matrix (ECM)

### 5.2 The Encapsulation Theory

Linus Pauling and Matthias Rath hypothesised that:

- Cancer cells spread by **dissolving the ECM** using matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and plasmin
- Adequate Vitamin C promotes strong collagen production, creating a **physical barrier** around tumours
- This **encapsulation** restricts tumour invasion and metastasis
- Vitamin C also inhibits MMP activity, reducing the tumour's ability to degrade surrounding tissue

### 5.3 Modern Validation

While the original Pauling-Rath hypothesis was controversial, modern research confirms:

- Vitamin C deficiency weakens the ECM and correlates with increased tumour invasiveness
- Adequate Vitamin C supports **stromal fibrosis** (fibrous tissue encapsulation of tumours)
- Enhanced collagen integrity restricts cancer cell migration through tissue planes

## 6. Immune System Activation

Vitamin C is found at very high concentrations within immune cells (10–100× plasma levels), reflecting its critical role in immune function.

### 6.1 Natural Killer (NK) Cell Enhancement

- Stimulates **NK cell proliferation** and cytotoxic activity
- NK cells are the immune system's first line of defence against cancer — they kill without prior sensitisation
- Vitamin C increases NK cell number and tumour-killing capacity

### 6.2 T Cell Support

| Function | Vitamin C Role |
|---|---|
| **T cell maturation** | Essential for differentiation of bone marrow progenitors into functional T lymphocytes |
| **T cell proliferation** | Promotes rapid clonal expansion in response to tumour antigens |
| **Epigenetic reprogramming** | Enhances TET-mediated demethylation in T cells, improving their anti-tumour function |
| **Prevention of exhaustion** | Helps maintain T cell function in the immunosuppressive tumour microenvironment |

### 6.3 Enhanced Immunotherapy Response

High-dose Vitamin C has been shown to:

- **Potentiate PD-1 blockade** (immune checkpoint inhibitors)
- Increase infiltration of **macrophages and CD8⁺ T cells** into tumours
- Boost **granzyme B production** (the killing molecule used by cytotoxic T cells)
- Lead to significant **tumour regression** when combined with anti-PD-1 therapy

### 6.4 Interferon & Cytokine Modulation

- Enhances production of **interferons** (IFN-α, IFN-γ) — key antiviral and anti-tumour signalling proteins
- Supports **cytokine production** by white blood cells during immune activation
- Modulates inflammatory response — reduces excessive pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α) while supporting anti-inflammatory IL-10

> **Key insight:** Vitamin C doesn't just directly kill cancer — it arms the immune system to recognise and destroy tumour cells more effectively, and enhances the response to modern immunotherapies.

## 7. Synergy with Conventional Therapies

### 7.1 Chemotherapy Enhancement

High-dose IV Vitamin C has demonstrated synergy with multiple chemotherapy agents:

| Chemotherapy Agent | Cancer Type | Observed Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Gemcitabine + nab-paclitaxel** | Metastatic pancreatic | Doubled overall survival (8 → 16 months) |
| **Etoposide** | Various | Increased cancer cell susceptibility |
| **Cisplatin** | Various | Enhanced cytotoxicity |
| **Doxorubicin** | Various | Synergistic cell killing; reduced muscle atrophy side effect |
| **FOLFOX** | Colorectal (RAS-mutant subgroup) | Improved progression-free survival |

### 7.2 Radiation Therapy Enhancement

- Acts as a **radiosensitiser** — makes cancer cells more vulnerable to radiation damage
- Simultaneously **protects normal tissue** from radiation-induced oxidative damage (via antioxidant action at physiological concentrations in healthy cells)
- Reduces radiation side effects: oral mucositis, xerostomia (dry mouth), cardiac dysfunction

### 7.3 Reduced Treatment Toxicity

- Decreases **chemotherapy-related fatigue**
- Reduces **nausea and treatment-related adverse events**
- Improves **quality of life** scores during treatment
- May allow patients to **tolerate higher effective doses** of chemotherapy

## 9. Administration & Dosing

### 9.1 Oral vs IV — The Critical Distinction

| Parameter | Oral Vitamin C | IV Vitamin C |
|---|---|---|
| **Max plasma concentration** | ~220 µmol/L (tightly regulated by gut absorption) | **14,000–15,000+ µmol/L** (bypasses absorption limits) |
| **Fold difference** | Baseline | **100–500× higher** than oral |
| **Pro-oxidant effect** | ❌ Not achievable | ✅ Cytotoxic H₂O₂ generation at >1,000 µmol/L |
| **Cancer cell killing** | ❌ Insufficient concentration | ✅ Selective cytotoxicity |
| **Use case** | Immune support, antioxidant, maintenance | Pharmacological anti-cancer therapy |

### 9.2 Dosing Protocols (Integrative Oncology)

| Route | Dose | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **IV (clinical)** | 25–100 g per infusion (1–1.5 g/kg) | 2–3× per week, 1–3 hour infusions | Standard integrative oncology protocol |
| **Oral liposomal** | 3–10 g/day in divided doses | Daily (off-IV days or maintenance) | ~1.77× bioavailability vs standard oral; does NOT achieve cytotoxic levels |
| **Standard oral** | 1–3 g/day | Daily | Immune support / baseline antioxidant only |

### 9.3 Important Safety Considerations

- **G6PD screening required** before high-dose IV — G6PD deficiency can cause haemolytic anaemia
- **Oxalate kidney stones** — risk with very high doses; adequate hydration essential
- High-dose IVC is generally well-tolerated with minimal side effects
- Should be administered under medical supervision
- **Timing with chemotherapy** should be coordinated — some oncologists advise separating IVC from chemo by 24–48 hours to avoid potential antioxidant interference (though evidence suggests synergy, not interference, at pharmacological doses)

```
HIGH-DOSE VITAMIN C
│
├── PRO-OXIDANT (H2O2 generation)

├── EPIGENETIC (TET2 activation)
│   └── DNA demethylation → gene reactivation

```
