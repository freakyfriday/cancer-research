# Ivermectin — Immune Activation & Anti-Cancer Mechanisms

> **Focus:** How Ivermectin signals killer T cells (CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes) to recognise and attack cancer cells, and the broader anti-tumour pathways it engages.

---

## 1. The Core Mechanism — Immunogenic Cell Death (ICD)

Cancer cells normally evade the immune system by dying "silently" (via tolerogenic apoptosis). Ivermectin forces them to die **loudly** — a process called **Immunogenic Cell Death (ICD)** — which alerts the immune system and triggers a targeted attack.

### 1.1 The Three Danger Signals (DAMPs)

When Ivermectin induces ICD in cancer cells, three critical **damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs)** are released:

| Signal | What It Does | Immune Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Calreticulin (CRT)** | Translocates from the ER to the cancer cell surface | Acts as an "eat me" flag — dendritic cells phagocytose (engulf) the dying cancer cell |
| **ATP** | Released into the extracellular space | Recruits dendritic cells and activates the NLRP3 inflammasome via P2X7 receptors |
| **HMGB1** | Released from the nucleus | Binds TLR4 on dendritic cells, promoting antigen presentation to T cells |

**Net effect:** Dendritic cells engulf the dying cancer cell, process its antigens, and **present them to CD8+ killer T cells**, which then hunt and destroy remaining cancer cells bearing those antigens.

### 1.2 The P2X7 / Pannexin-1 Axis

Ivermectin is a **positive allosteric modulator** of the ATP/P2X4/P2X7/Pannexin-1 axis:

1. Ivermectin enhances P2X4 receptor signalling
2. P2X4 collaborates with P2X7 and Pannexin-1 channels
3. This amplifies ATP release and inflammasome activation in myeloid cells
4. The tumour microenvironment already has high extracellular ATP — Ivermectin **potentiates** this existing signal

The P2X7 receptor is expressed predominantly on **dendritic cells, macrophages, and monocytes**. A functional P2X7 receptor is crucial for initiating the anti-tumour immune response — T lymphocytes are activated against tumour cells specifically by P2X7R-expressing dendritic cells.

> **Key insight:** Ivermectin doesn't just kill cancer cells — it kills them in a way that *generates a vaccine-like immune response* against the tumour.

## 2. Cold → Hot Tumour Conversion

One of the most significant findings is Ivermectin's ability to convert **immunologically "cold" tumours** (poor T cell infiltration, unresponsive to immunotherapy) into **"hot" tumours** (robust immune infiltration, responsive to checkpoint inhibitors).

### What Changes in the Tumour Microenvironment

| Metric | Before Ivermectin | After Ivermectin |
|---|---|---|
| CD8+ killer T cells | Low / absent | Significantly increased infiltration |
| CD4+ helper T cells | Low | Increased |
| Regulatory T cells (Tregs) | High (immunosuppressive) | Reduced |
| Teff : Treg ratio | Low (immune suppression wins) | High (immune attack dominates) |
| Myeloid suppressor cells | High | Selectively targeted and reduced |
| Response to anti-PD1 | Poor | Significantly enhanced |

### How This Happens

1. **ICD** releases DAMPs (calreticulin, ATP, HMGB1)
2. **Dendritic cells** mature and present tumour antigens
3. **CD8+ T cells** are primed and infiltrate the tumour
4. **Immunosuppressive cells** (Tregs, myeloid-derived suppressor cells) are selectively depleted
5. The tumour becomes visible to the immune system and vulnerable to attack

## 3. Synergy with Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (Anti-PD1)

Preclinical studies (particularly in breast cancer models) show that **Ivermectin + anti-PD1** produces dramatically better outcomes than either agent alone:

- **Significant tumour growth reduction**
- **Complete responses** in some metastatic disease models
- **Protective immunity** — cured animals resist re-challenge with the same cancer (immune memory)
- **Reduced relapse** rates
- Generation of **tumour-reactive CD8+ T cells** that persist long-term

### Why This Works

PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors work by removing the "brakes" on T cells. But they **require T cells to already be present** in the tumour. Ivermectin solves the upstream problem: it **drives T cell infiltration** into cold tumours, giving checkpoint inhibitors something to work with.

## 4. Additional Anti-Cancer Pathways

Beyond immune activation, Ivermectin attacks cancer through multiple parallel mechanisms:

### 4.1 WNT-TCF Pathway Inhibition
- Represses WNT-TCF target gene expression in colorectal, lung, and other cancers
- Reduces β-catenin and CYCLIN D1 levels
- **Inhibits cancer stem cell self-renewal** (blocks NANOG, SOX2, OCT4)
- More effective against breast cancer stem cells than paclitaxel in vitro

### 4.2 Akt/mTOR Pathway Suppression
- Blocks PI3K/Akt/mTOR signalling across breast, glioma, pancreatic, ovarian, and lung cancers
- Promotes PAK1 ubiquitination and degradation
- Induces cytostatic autophagy (cancer cells stop dividing and self-digest)

### 4.3 Mitochondrial Dysfunction & Oxidative Stress
- Inhibits mitochondrial complex I → disrupts electron transport chain
- Reduces mitochondrial membrane potential and ATP production
- Generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) → DNA damage → apoptosis
- **Selectively affects cancer cells** while sparing normal cells (cancer cells are already under metabolic stress)

### 4.4 Multi-Drug Resistance Reversal
- Inhibits P-glycoprotein (P-gp), the drug efflux pump that cancer cells use to expel chemotherapy
- May sensitise resistant tumours to conventional treatments

## 5. Clinical Status & Dosing

### 5.1 Current Clinical Trials

| Trial | Phase | Cancer Type | Design |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NCT05318469** (Cedars-Sinai) | Phase I/II | Metastatic triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) | Ivermectin + balstilimab (anti-PD-1), 21-day cycles |
| **NCT02366884** (Dr. Frank Arguello) | Phase II | Advanced / metastatic (various) | "Atavistic chemotherapy" protocol including Ivermectin |
| **NCI Preclinical Study** | Preclinical | Various | Results anticipated early 2026 |

### 5.2 Dosing Notes

| Context | Dose |
|---|---|
| Standard antiparasitic | 150–400 µg/kg (single dose) |
| Cancer protocols (integrative) | Up to 24 mg/day, 6 days/week |
| Aggressive cancer protocols | Up to 1 mg/kg/day |
| **This protocol (Ignazio)** | **120 mg/day (10 × 12 mg tablets) with ~50 g fat** |

> ⚠️ **Important:** The protocol dose of 120 mg/day is significantly higher than standard antiparasitic dosing. Fat co-administration is critical for bioavailability (Ivermectin is highly lipophilic).

### 5.3 Safety Profile

- A review of 36 cancer patients found Ivermectin consistently well-tolerated
- No serious adverse events attributed to the drug
- Minor side effects: mild GI upset, transient rash
- Safely administered alongside active chemotherapy
- **FDA caution:** High doses can potentially cause seizures, coma, or death — medical supervision is essential

## 6. Summary — The Immune Activation Cascade

```
Ivermectin
    │
    ▼
Cancer Cell undergoes Immunogenic Cell Death (ICD)
    │
    ├── Calreticulin → "eat me" signal on cell surface
    ├── ATP release → P2X7 receptor → dendritic cell recruitment
    └── HMGB1 release → TLR4 → dendritic cell maturation
            │
            ▼
    Dendritic cells present tumour antigens
            │
            ▼
    CD8+ Killer T Cells are primed & activated
            │
            ▼
    T cells infiltrate tumour (cold → hot conversion)
            │
            ├── Tregs & myeloid suppressor cells reduced
            └── Teff:Treg ratio increases
                    │
                    ▼
            Tumour becomes vulnerable to immune attack
            (+ enhanced response to anti-PD1 immunotherapy)
```
