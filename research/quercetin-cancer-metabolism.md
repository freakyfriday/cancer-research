# Quercetin — Dismantling Cancer Metabolism

> **Focus:** How Quercetin — a plant flavonoid — attacks cancer at its metabolic core: starving tumours of glucose, collapsing key survival pathways, reprogramming lipid and amino acid metabolism, rewriting the epigenetic landscape, and triggering multiple forms of cell death — while leaving normal cells largely unharmed.

---

## 1. The Core Attack — Reversing the Warburg Effect

Cancer cells hijack glucose metabolism through the **Warburg effect** — preferring rapid, inefficient glycolysis over mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, even when oxygen is plentiful. This generates the building blocks and energy needed for explosive growth. Quercetin dismantles this dependency at every level.

### 1.1 Hexokinase II (HK2) Suppression

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Normal cancer cell | HK2 — the first and rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis — is massively overexpressed, trapping glucose inside the cell |
| + Quercetin | HK2 expression is **downregulated**, blocking the very first step of glycolysis |
| Result | Glucose cannot be phosphorylated or metabolised — the entire glycolytic cascade stalls |

HK2 also anchors to the outer mitochondrial membrane, where it **physically blocks apoptosis** by preventing cytochrome c release. When Quercetin reduces HK2, this anti-death shield is removed simultaneously.

### 1.2 Glucose Uptake Blockade

- Quercetin **decreases glucose uptake** by cancer cells, cutting fuel supply before it even enters glycolysis
- **Lactate production drops** — a direct marker that aerobic glycolysis (the Warburg effect) is being reversed
- This mimics the effect of glucose deprivation, forcing cancer cells into metabolic crisis

### 1.3 Mitochondrial Dysfunction

Quercetin doesn't just block glycolysis — it attacks the backup energy system too:

- **Basal respiration** is reduced — mitochondria can't maintain normal energy output
- **Maximal respiratory capacity** is suppressed — cells have no metabolic reserve
- **ATP production collapses** — the cell's energy currency dries up
- Direct inhibition of **mitochondrial ATPase** activity delivers the final bioenergetic blow

> **Key insight:** Quercetin creates a metabolic pincer — blocking glycolysis from the top (HK2, glucose uptake) while simultaneously collapsing mitochondrial function from below. The cancer cell has no escape route for energy production.

## 2. Signalling Pathway Shutdown — Cutting the Command Lines

Cancer cells rely on hyperactivated survival signalling pathways. Quercetin acts as a **multi-pathway inhibitor**, simultaneously disrupting the command structure that keeps cancer cells growing, dividing, and evading death.

### 2.1 PI3K/Akt/mTOR — The Master Growth Axis

This is arguably the most important survival pathway in cancer, driving growth, proliferation, and treatment resistance.

| Target | Quercetin's Action | Downstream Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **PI3K** | Decreases enzymatic activity and expression | Growth signalling silenced at the source |
| **Akt** | Suppresses phosphorylation (activation) | Survival signals cannot be transmitted |
| **mTOR** | Dual-specific inhibitor (blocks both mTOR and PI3K) | Protein synthesis and cell growth halt |

Quercetin's mTOR inhibition occurs through three independent mechanisms:
1. **PI3K-dependent Akt suppression** — cuts the canonical activation route
2. **AMPK activation** — the cell's energy sensor detects metabolic stress and further suppresses mTOR
3. **Hamartin upregulation** — a natural mTOR brake is re-engaged

### 2.2 Wnt/β-Catenin — The Proliferation Switch

- Quercetin **reduces β-catenin stabilisation**, preventing it from entering the nucleus
- Nuclear β-catenin drives transcription of proliferation genes (cyclin D1, c-Myc)
- By blocking this, Quercetin shuts down a key cancer proliferation programme
- Also suppresses β-catenin indirectly via **upregulation of miR-320a**

### 2.3 MAPK/ERK1/2 — The Growth Signal Relay

- Quercetin **inhibits ERK phosphorylation**, preventing activation of this proliferation cascade
- The MAPK/ERK pathway is often constitutively active in cancers with RAS mutations
- Inhibiting this pathway reduces cell proliferation and enhances sensitivity to apoptosis

### 2.4 HIF-1α — The Hypoxia Survival Factor

- Quercetin **reduces HIF-1α stabilisation** — the master transcription factor that helps cancers survive low oxygen
- HIF-1α drives the expression of GLUT transporters, glycolytic enzymes, and VEGF (angiogenesis)
- Suppressing HIF-1α reinforces the metabolic attack (less glucose uptake) and anti-angiogenic effects

### 2.5 Additional Pathways

| Pathway | Quercetin's Effect |
|---|---|
| **NF-κB** | Suppressed — reduces inflammation-driven survival and chemo-resistance |
| **JAK/STAT** | Inhibited — blocks cytokine-driven proliferation signals |
| **p53** | Phosphorylated and stabilised — activates the master tumour suppressor |
| **Foxo3a** | Activated in triple-negative breast cancer — promotes apoptosis genes |

> **Key insight:** Quercetin doesn't target one pathway — it simultaneously dismantles at least 7 major survival and growth signalling axes. This "multi-hit" approach makes it extremely difficult for cancer cells to develop resistance through single-pathway escape.

## 3. Lipid & Fatty Acid Metabolism — Cutting the Building Materials

Cancer cells don't just need glucose — they need lipids for new membranes, signalling molecules, and energy. Quercetin attacks this dependency too.

### 3.1 Fatty Acid Oxidation (FAO) Inhibition

- Quercetin acts as a **"[silent retarder](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35511410/)"** of fatty acid oxidation in breast cancer
- Targets **CPT1 (carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1)** — the rate-limiting enzyme that shuttles fatty acids into mitochondria for burning
- Blocking CPT1 cuts off a major alternative ATP source that metastatic breast cancer cells rely on
- This is critical because many aggressive cancers switch to FAO when glycolysis is blocked — Quercetin closes this escape route

### 3.2 Fatty Acid Synthase (FASN) Inhibition

- Quercetin inhibits **FASN** in liver cancer cells (HepG2)
- FASN is the enzyme responsible for *de novo* fatty acid synthesis — cancer cells massively upregulate it
- Blocking FASN deprives cancer cells of the lipids needed for membrane synthesis and signalling
- This inhibition directly triggers **apoptosis**

### 3.3 AMPK Activation

- Quercetin activates **AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK)** — the master metabolic sensor
- AMPK activation shifts the cell from anabolic (growth) to catabolic (survival) mode
- In cancer cells already under metabolic stress from Quercetin's other attacks, AMPK activation amplifies the crisis

---

## 4. Glutamine Metabolism — Blocking the Backup Fuel

Many cancers are "glutamine-addicted" — they rely on glutamine as a secondary carbon and nitrogen source when glucose metabolism is disrupted. Quercetin cuts this lifeline too.

### 4.1 SLC1A5 Transporter Downregulation

- Quercetin **downregulates SLC1A5** (also called ASCT2) — the primary glutamine transporter on cancer cell membranes
- Less SLC1A5 = less glutamine entering the cell
- This mechanism is particularly powerful in **overcoming multi-drug resistance** in colon cancer — drug-resistant cells that have shifted to glutamine dependency are re-sensitised

### 4.2 GDH1 (Glutamate Dehydrogenase 1) Inhibition

- Quercetin **inhibits GDH1**, the enzyme that converts glutamate (from glutamine) into α-ketoglutarate for the TCA cycle
- Blocking GDH1 prevents cancer cells from using glutamine to fuel mitochondrial energy production
- This triggers **apoptosis in hepatocellular carcinoma** cells

> **Key insight:** By attacking glucose (glycolysis + mitochondria), lipid (FAO + FASN), and glutamine (SLC1A5 + GDH1) metabolism simultaneously, Quercetin executes a **complete metabolic siege** — every major fuel source is compromised at once.

## 5. ROS Modulation — The Pro-Oxidant Paradox

Quercetin is famous as an antioxidant. In cancer cells, it does the opposite.

### 5.1 The Dual Nature

| Context | Quercetin's Role |
|---|---|
| Normal cells | Acts as an **antioxidant** — scavenges ROS, protects DNA |
| Cancer cells | Acts as a **pro-oxidant** — generates lethal ROS levels |

This selectivity is key to Quercetin's safety profile.

### 5.2 How It Becomes Pro-Oxidant in Cancer

1. Quercetin is oxidised into **quercetin-semiquinones** and **quercetin-quinones** inside cancer cells
2. These reactive metabolites **deplete glutathione (GSH)** — the cell's primary antioxidant defence
3. Cancer cells already operate at elevated baseline ROS levels (due to their hyperactive metabolism)
4. With GSH depleted, ROS accumulates to **lethal levels**
5. Oxidative damage to DNA, lipids, and proteins triggers apoptosis

### 5.3 Why Cancer Cells Can't Cope

- Normal cells maintain a comfortable ROS buffer — Quercetin's antioxidant effects keep them safe
- Cancer cells live on a razor's edge of oxidative stress — Quercetin tips them over
- This is the same selectivity mechanism exploited by **high-dose vitamin C** (which generates H₂O₂ selectively in cancer cells)

## 6. Epigenetic Reprogramming — Rewriting Cancer's Instruction Manual

Perhaps Quercetin's most remarkable anti-cancer mechanism: it **reverses the epigenetic changes** that silence tumour suppressor genes.

### 6.1 HDAC Inhibition (Histone Deacetylases)

- Quercetin **dose-dependently decreases HDAC activity** — acts as a competitive inhibitor at the catalytic site
- HDACs remove acetyl groups from histones → tightens chromatin → silences gene expression
- By inhibiting HDACs → histones remain acetylated → chromatin opens → **tumour suppressor genes are reactivated**
- Specifically: increases **histone H3 and H4 acetylation**, promoting gene expression of apoptotic mediators
- Reduces survivin expression in prostate cancer by modulating HDAC-mediated H3 acetylation
- Increases **Fas ligand** expression through H3 acetylation, enabling immune-mediated killing

### 6.2 DNMT Inhibition (DNA Methyltransferases)

- Quercetin **decreases DNMT activity** in a dose-dependent manner (DNMT1, DNMT3a)
- DNMTs add methyl groups to CpG islands in gene promoters → silences tumour suppressors
- By inhibiting DNMTs → **promoter methylation is reversed** → tumour suppressor genes are reactivated
- Specifically: demethylation of **P16INK4a** promoter → restores cell cycle control
- Global DNA methylation levels decrease with increasing Quercetin dose and exposure time

### 6.3 MicroRNA Modulation

Quercetin modulates the expression of cancer-relevant microRNAs:

| miRNA | Quercetin's Effect | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **miR-26b** ↑ | Tumour-suppressive miRNA restored | Oncogenic targets suppressed |
| **miR-126** ↑ | Anti-angiogenic miRNA increased | Blood vessel formation reduced |
| **miR-320a** ↑ | Suppresses β-catenin | Wnt proliferation pathway blocked |
| **miR-34a-5p** ↑ | Tumour suppressor miRNA restored | Apoptosis pathways re-engaged |
| **miR-21** ↓ | Oncogenic miRNA reduced | Pro-survival signalling weakened |

> **Key insight:** Quercetin doesn't just kill cancer cells — it **reprogrammes their gene expression** back towards a normal phenotype by reversing the epigenetic silencing of tumour suppressor genes. This is a fundamentally different mechanism from most anti-cancer agents.

## 7. Cell Death — Multiple Kill Pathways

Quercetin triggers cancer cell death through multiple independent mechanisms:

### 7.1 Apoptosis (Programmed Cell Death)

**Intrinsic (mitochondrial) pathway:**
- Ca²⁺-mediated dissipation of mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm)
- Cytochrome c release → caspase-9 → caspase-3 activation → PARP cleavage
- BAX upregulation + Bcl-2 downregulation shifts the balance toward death

**Extrinsic (death receptor) pathway:**
- Caspase-8 activation → caspase-3 cascade
- Fas ligand upregulation (via HDAC inhibition / H3 acetylation)

**p53-dependent amplification:**
- p53 is phosphorylated and stabilised
- p53 → p21 upregulation → cell cycle arrest
- p53 → cyclin D1 suppression → blocks G1/S transition

### 7.2 Autophagy

- Quercetin induces autophagy by **inactivating Akt-mTOR** and suppressing **HIF-1α**
- In gastric and breast cancer cells: protective autophagy is initially triggered
- In lung cancer cells: autophagy is cytotoxic, mediated by **SIRT1/AMPK** signalling
- Quercetin enhances **TRAIL-mediated tumour cell death** through autophagy flux activation — making cells more sensitive to immune killing

### 7.3 Cell Cycle Arrest

- Arrests cancer cells at **G1/S** and **G2/M** checkpoints
- p21 and p27 upregulation → CDK inhibition → cell cycle stalls
- Cyclin D1 suppression prevents G1/S transition
- Dividing cancer cells are halted and become vulnerable to apoptosis

## 8. Anti-Angiogenesis & Anti-Metastasis

### 8.1 Cutting the Blood Supply

- Quercetin **suppresses VEGF secretion** — the primary signal for new blood vessel growth
- Blocks **VEGFR-2** signalling and its downstream **Akt/mTOR/P70S6K** cascade
- Inhibits endothelial cell proliferation, migration, invasion, and tube formation
- Downregulates **MALAT1** and **MIAT** long non-coding RNAs involved in angiogenesis
- Inhibits **calcineurin activity** and its downstream angiogenic pathways in breast cancer
- Reduces **MMP (matrix metalloproteinase)** levels — enzymes that chew through tissue barriers

### 8.2 Blocking Metastasis

- Suppresses **epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT)** — the process by which cancer cells become mobile and invasive
- Reduces MMP activity → tumour cells cannot degrade basement membrane to invade tissues
- Anti-angiogenic effects limit the vascular routes available for distant spread

## 9. Synergy with Other Protocol Compounds

Quercetin's multi-target action creates powerful synergies with other compounds in the protocol:

### 9.1 Chemotherapy Sensitisation

| Drug | Quercetin's Synergistic Effect |
|---|---|
| **5-Fluorouracil** | Enhances cytotoxicity, helps overcome drug resistance |
| **Cisplatin** | Increases cancer cell sensitivity |
| **Doxorubicin** | Potentiates anti-tumour effects + may reduce cardiotoxicity |
| **Paclitaxel** | Enhances efficacy |

Quercetin may also **reduce chemotherapy side effects** — including cardiotoxicity, nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, and GI issues — while potentiating anti-cancer effects.

### 9.2 Protocol Compound Synergies

| Compound | Shared/Complementary Mechanisms |
|---|---|
| **Fenbendazole** | Both target HK2 and glucose metabolism; Quercetin adds PI3K/Akt/mTOR inhibition on top of Fenbendazole's tubulin disruption |
| **Ivermectin** | Both suppress Wnt/β-catenin and PI3K/Akt/mTOR; Quercetin's epigenetic modulation complements Ivermectin's immune activation |
| **Vitamin C (high-dose)** | Both exploit the pro-oxidant paradox in cancer cells — Quercetin depletes GSH while Vitamin C generates H₂O₂; combined ROS assault is amplified |
| **Resveratrol** | Both activate AMPK/SIRT1 and modulate autophagy; complementary epigenetic effects (HDAC/DNMT inhibition) |

### 9.3 CYP450 Interactions — Caution

- Quercetin can inhibit **CYP3A4** and **CYP2C19** enzymes *in vitro*
- These enzymes metabolise many drugs, including some chemotherapy agents
- Potential to **increase blood levels** of co-administered drugs
- Clinical significance in humans is still being established but warrants awareness

## 10. Clinical Evidence & Dosing

### 10.1 Human Clinical Trials

| Trial | Cancer Type | Status |
|---|---|---|
| NCI-listed trials | Ovarian, glioma, multiple myeloma | Active |
| NCT04252625 (Phase 2) | Prostate (radiation-related) | Completed Dec 2023 |
| Various studies | Breast, lung, prostate, hepatocellular, lymphoma | Preclinical/early clinical |

### 10.2 Dosing in Studies

| Context | Dose |
|---|---|
| Human blood pressure studies | 162–1,095 mg/day (7 days – 6 weeks) |
| Prostate cancer study | 800 mg quercetin + 1 g green tea extract |
| In vitro anti-cancer effects | 10–500 µM (varies by cancer type) |
| Low-dose breast cancer inhibition | 1–20 µM (without cytotoxicity to normal cells) |

### 10.3 Bioavailability — The Key Challenge

Quercetin has **poor oral bioavailability** and low aqueous solubility:

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Quercetin glucosides (food form) | Peak plasma: 3.5–7.8 µmol/L |
| Unconjugated quercetin (supplement form) | Peak plasma: <0.33 µmol/L |
| **Bioavailability enhancers** | Whole foods, isoquercitrin conjugates, lipid formulations, solid dispersants |

> **Protocol consideration:** Taking Quercetin with dietary fat and/or in combination with bioavailability-enhancing formulations (phytosomal / liposomal quercetin) can significantly improve absorption.

### 10.4 Safety Profile

- Human clinical trials have reported **no toxic side effects** at standard supplemental doses
- At low doses (≤500 mg/day): primarily acts as antioxidant
- At higher doses / in cancer cells: pro-oxidant and chemotherapeutic effects emerge
- **Dose-dependent dual behaviour**: antioxidant at low concentrations, pro-oxidant at higher concentrations — the latter is the desired anti-cancer effect

> **Why this matters for the protocol:** Quercetin is a **metabolic Swiss army knife** — it attacks cancer through at least 8 distinct mechanisms simultaneously. Its unique strengths — particularly the PI3K/Akt/mTOR inhibition, epigenetic reprogramming, and the pro-oxidant paradox — complement the structural destruction of Fenbendazole, the immune activation of Ivermectin, and the ROS generation of high-dose Vitamin C. Together, these compounds create an overwhelming multi-front assault that makes it extraordinarily difficult for cancer cells to adapt or resist.
