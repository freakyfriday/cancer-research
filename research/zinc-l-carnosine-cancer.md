# Zinc-L-Carnosine (ZnC) — The Gut Shield That Fights Cancer

> **Focus:** How Zinc-L-Carnosine — a chelated compound of zinc and L-carnosine — attacks colorectal cancer through direct anti-tumour effects, immune system reprogramming, gut barrier fortification, metabolic disruption, DNA damage prevention, and chemotherapy protection — while simultaneously maintaining the gut mucosal environment that prevents tumorigenesis in the first place.

---

## 1. What Is Zinc-L-Carnosine?

Zinc-L-Carnosine (ZnC), also known as **polaprezinc** or **PepZin GI™**, is a chelated compound that pairs one zinc ion with one molecule of L-carnosine (β-alanyl-L-histidine). This is not simply "zinc plus carnosine" — the chelation creates a distinct molecular entity with properties superior to either component alone:

| Property | Zinc Alone | L-Carnosine Alone | Zinc-L-Carnosine |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gastric residence time | Rapidly absorbed | Rapidly broken down by carnosinase | **Extended release** — stays in the stomach/gut lining 3× longer |
| Mucosal adherence | Low | Low | **High** — adheres to injured mucosa |
| Anti-inflammatory potency | Moderate | Moderate | **Synergistic** — exceeds both combined |
| Anti-ulcer efficacy | Some | Some | **Superior** — approved drug in Japan for gastric ulcers |

> **Key insight:** The chelation is what makes ZnC special. It delivers zinc directly to the gut lining with sustained release, creating a localised protective and anti-cancer environment exactly where colon cancer develops.

## 2. Why Zinc Matters — The Cancer Connection

Zinc is the second most abundant trace mineral in the human body and is required by over 300 enzymes and 2,000+ transcription factors. Its relationship to cancer — especially colorectal cancer — is profound.

### 2.1 Zinc Deficiency Drives Colorectal Tumorigenesis

| Observation | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Zinc concentration **drops** during the transition from normal colon mucosa → precancerous lesions → cancer | Reduced zinc impairs zinc-dependent tumour suppressors and DNA repair enzymes |
| Dietary zinc deficiency **increases** expression of COX-2, TNF-α, CCL, CXCL, and IL chemokines | Chronic pro-inflammatory environment promotes tumour initiation |
| Lower serum zinc levels correlate with **poorer prognosis** in CRC patients | Zinc depletion impairs anti-tumour immune responses |
| A daily 5 mg increment of dietary zinc is associated with a **14% lower risk** of colorectal cancer (meta-analysis) | Adequate zinc supports immune surveillance and DNA integrity |

### 2.2 Zinc Transporter Dysregulation in Colon Cancer

Cancer cells manipulate zinc levels by altering the expression of two transporter families:

**ZIP transporters (SLC39A) — Zinc IN to cytoplasm:**
- **ZIP7** is overexpressed in CRC → activates MAPK, PI3K, and mTOR → promotes proliferation
- **ZIP4** overexpression → linked to reduced survival in CRC
- **ZIP6** upregulated → promotes proliferation, migration, and apoptosis resistance

**ZnT transporters (SLC30) — Zinc OUT of cytoplasm:**
- **ZnT1** upregulation → lowers tissue zinc → contributes to zinc-depleted tumour microenvironment
- **ZnT5, ZnT6, ZnT7** upregulated in CRC → complex, context-dependent roles

> **Key insight:** Colorectal cancer actively disrupts zinc homeostasis to create a zinc-depleted environment that favours tumour growth. Zinc-L-Carnosine supplementation directly counters this by restoring zinc to the gut mucosa.

## 3. Direct Anti-Tumour Effects — Killing Cancer Cells

ZnC has been shown to **directly inhibit the proliferation, invasion, and migration** of colorectal cancer cells (HCT116, LOVO, HT29, LIM2045 cell lines) both in vitro and in vivo.

### 3.1 NF-κB/STAT1 Pathway Suppression

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Normal cancer cell | NF-κB and STAT1 are hyperactivated → drive inflammation, survival, and proliferation |
| + ZnC | Phosphorylation of **STAT1** and **NF-κB p65** is significantly inhibited |
| Result | Inflammatory survival signalling collapses → cell cycle arrest and apoptosis are triggered |

NF-κB suppression has cascading downstream effects:
- **IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α** expression drops → removes inflammatory fuel for tumour growth
- Pro-survival gene transcription is silenced
- Cancer cells become vulnerable to immune attack

### 3.2 Wnt/β-Catenin/Tcf-4 Signalling Shutdown

The Wnt pathway is one of the most frequently mutated pathways in colorectal cancer (>90% of CRC cases have Wnt pathway activation):

- L-carnosine (the carnosine component of ZnC) **reduces β-catenin and Tcf-4 expression**
- β-catenin/Tcf-4 nuclear complex drives transcription of cyclin D1, c-Myc → cell proliferation
- Suppressing this pathway directly **inhibits the core proliferation programme** of CRC cells
- This is particularly significant because APC mutations (common in CRC) constitutively activate Wnt signalling

### 3.3 HIF-1α Degradation — Dismantling Hypoxia Survival

Tumour cores are chronically hypoxic. Cancer cells survive this through HIF-1α:

- L-carnosine **reduces HIF-1α protein levels** and transcriptional activity
- Promotes HIF-1α degradation via the **ubiquitin-proteasome pathway**
- HIF-1α drives expression of VEGF (angiogenesis), GLUT1 (glucose import), and glycolytic enzymes
- Degrading HIF-1α simultaneously attacks **angiogenesis**, **glucose metabolism**, and **drug resistance**

### 3.4 Apoptosis Induction — Multiple Kill Mechanisms

L-carnosine triggers cancer cell death through several pathways simultaneously:

| Mechanism | Key Players |
|---|---|
| **Intrinsic apoptosis** | BAX ↑, Bcl-2 ↓ → mitochondrial membrane permeabilisation → cleaved caspase-3 activation |
| **Cell cycle arrest** | p53 ↑, p21 ↑, cyclin D1 modulation → cells stall at checkpoints and enter apoptosis |
| **Necroptosis** | Non-apoptotic programmed cell death also induced — a backup kill pathway |
| **Autophagy** | Cytotoxic autophagy triggered in cancer cells under metabolic stress |

> **Key insight:** ZnC attacks CRC cells through at least 4 major signalling axes (NF-κB/STAT1, Wnt/β-catenin, HIF-1α, and p53/caspase apoptosis) simultaneously. This multi-pathway approach makes resistance extremely difficult.

## 4. Immune System Reprogramming — Unleashing Anti-Tumour Immunity

Perhaps the most exciting recent discovery is ZnC's ability to **reshape the tumour immune microenvironment** from immunosuppressive to immunoactive.

### 4.1 The miR-570/PD-L1 Axis

ZnC up-regulates **PD-L1** expression on cancer cells by inhibiting **miR-570**:

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| 1 | ZnC inhibits miR-570 in CRC cells |
| 2 | miR-570 normally suppresses PD-L1 → its inhibition causes PD-L1 to rise on cancer cell surfaces |
| 3 | Higher PD-L1 makes the cancer **more responsive to anti-PD1 immunotherapy** |
| 4 | ZnC + anti-PD1 combination → **synergistic anti-tumour response** in mouse CRC models |

### 4.2 Immune Cell Rebalancing

In vivo (mouse CRC models), ZnC treatment produced dramatic shifts in immune cell populations within the tumour:

| Cell Type | Direction | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **CD8+ T cells** (cytotoxic) | ↑ Increased | These are the primary tumour-killing immune cells |
| **Regulatory T cells (Tregs)** | ↓ Decreased | Tregs suppress anti-tumour immunity — removing them unleashes the immune response |
| **MDSCs** (myeloid-derived suppressor cells) | ↓ Decreased | MDSCs actively block immune attack on tumours |
| **M2 macrophages** (pro-tumour) | ↓ Decreased | M2 macrophages promote tumour growth and immune evasion |

### 4.3 Zinc's Role in Cytotoxic T Cell Function

Zinc itself is essential for T cell-mediated anti-tumour immunity:
- Adequate zinc **increases granzyme B transcription** — the enzyme CD8+ T cells use to kill cancer cells
- Zinc supports **thymic function** and T cell maturation
- Zinc deficiency impairs Natural Killer (NK) cell activity

> **Key insight:** ZnC simultaneously makes cancer cells more visible to the immune system (via PD-L1 upregulation) AND strengthens the immune army that attacks them (CD8+ T cells ↑, suppressors ↓). This is why ZnC + anti-PD1 therapy shows synergistic effects.

## 5. Metabolic Disruption — Starving Cancer Cells

L-carnosine disrupts cancer cell energy metabolism at multiple levels:

### 5.1 ATP Production Collapse

- L-carnosine **inhibits ATP production** in cancer cells, particularly through glycolytic pathways
- Reduces expression of glycolysis-related enzymes **GLUT1** (glucose transporter) and **PFK1** (phosphofructokinase 1)
- The glycolytic blockade starves cancer cells of their preferred rapid energy source

### 5.2 ROS Modulation

- ZnC influences **Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS)** production in cancer cells
- Cancer cells already operate at elevated baseline ROS → L-carnosine modulates ROS to push levels beyond the lethal threshold
- Simultaneously, ZnC's antioxidant properties protect normal cells from oxidative damage — a **selective pro-oxidant effect** similar to quercetin and high-dose vitamin C

### 5.3 Mitochondrial Respiration

- In gastric cancer cells, L-carnosine reduces both **mitochondrial respiration** and **glycolysis**
- This dual metabolic blockade leaves cancer cells with no viable energy production pathway
- Overall **cellular ATP content drops** — triggering metabolic crisis and cell death

> **Key insight:** L-carnosine creates a metabolic pincer — blocking glycolysis (the Warburg effect) while simultaneously impairing mitochondrial respiration. Cancer cells have no escape route for energy production.

## 6. Anti-Angiogenesis — Cutting the Blood Supply

Tumours cannot grow beyond ~2 mm without recruiting new blood vessels. ZnC attacks this process at multiple points:

| Target | ZnC/L-Carnosine's Action |
|---|---|
| **VEGF** | Expression reduced → primary angiogenic signal suppressed |
| **EGFR** | Levels decreased → growth factor signalling impaired |
| **HIF-1α** | Degraded → master regulator of angiogenic gene expression removed |
| **VEGFR-2 signalling** | Suppressed → endothelial cell proliferation, tube formation, migration, and invasion inhibited |
| **Microvessel density** | Reduced in tumours (shown in bladder cancer models) |

> By suppressing VEGF, EGFR, and HIF-1α simultaneously, ZnC executes a comprehensive anti-angiogenic programme that starves tumours of their blood supply.

## 7. Gut Barrier Protection — Preventing Cancer Before It Starts

ZnC's best-established clinical application is **gastrointestinal mucosal protection** — and this directly contributes to cancer prevention in the colon.

### 7.1 Tight Junction Integrity

- ZnC **stabilises tight-junction proteins** between epithelial cells
- Zinc is essential for the proper formation of tight-junction protein complexes
- Intact tight junctions prevent the translocation of toxins, bacteria, and inflammatory molecules across the gut wall
- Disrupted tight junctions ("leaky gut") create a chronic inflammatory state that promotes tumorigenesis

### 7.2 Mucosal Healing and Cell Migration

- ZnC **stimulates epithelial cell migration** and growth → faster healing of mucosal injuries
- Promotes **DNA replication and protein synthesis** required for tissue repair
- Stabilises cell membranes against damage
- Particularly effective at healing NSAID-induced gut injury (a risk factor for GI cancers)

### 7.3 The Anti-Inflammatory Shield

ZnC suppresses inflammatory mediators across the gut lining:

| Inflammatory Target | ZnC's Effect |
|---|---|
| **NF-κB** | Transcription suppressed → pro-inflammatory gene expression blocked |
| **IL-1β** | Reduced |
| **IL-6** | Reduced |
| **IL-8** | Reduced |
| **TNF-α** | Reduced |
| **COX-2** | Expression suppressed |

This is critical because **chronic inflammation is the single greatest risk factor for colon cancer** — conditions like ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease dramatically increase CRC risk. ZnC directly addresses the inflammatory microenvironment that gives rise to tumours.

### 7.4 Intestinal Permeability Reduction

- ZnC **prevents increased intestinal permeability** (leaky gut) caused by NSAIDs, stress, and strenuous exercise
- Reduced permeability means less translocation of bacterial endotoxins (LPS) across the gut wall
- LPS-driven systemic inflammation is a known promoter of tumorigenesis

> **Key insight:** ZnC doesn't just fight existing cancer — it **prevents the inflammatory, permeable gut environment** where colon cancer originates. This dual action (prevention + treatment) makes it uniquely valuable for CRC.

## 8. DNA Damage Protection — Genomic Guardian

Accumulation of DNA damage is the fundamental driver of carcinogenesis. ZnC provides multi-layered protection:

### 8.1 Protection Against Oxidative DNA Damage

- ZnC protects human **colon fibroblast cells** from hydrogen peroxide-induced oxidative stress and DNA damage
- Prevents **DNA strand breaks** and **micronucleus formation** (markers of chromosomal damage)
- Scavenges free radicals and ROS before they can reach DNA

### 8.2 The MTF-1/Metallothionein Pathway

- ZnC activates the **MTF-1 (Metal Transcription Factor-1)** signalling pathway
- MTF-1 induces **metallothionein (MT) expression** — a family of proteins that:
  - Bind and sequester toxic metals that cause DNA damage
  - Act as intracellular antioxidants
  - Protect against genotoxic substances
- This pathway provides **long-lasting genomic stability** rather than just acute protection

### 8.3 Antioxidant Enzyme Upregulation

- ZnC **upregulates erythrocyte superoxide dismutase (eSOD)** activity
- SOD is one of the body's primary antioxidant defences — converting superoxide radicals to hydrogen peroxide (which is then neutralised by catalase)
- Enhanced SOD activity creates a more stable, less mutagenic environment for gut tissue

> **Key insight:** ZnC protects the genome through three layers: direct ROS scavenging, MTF-1/metallothionein induction, and antioxidant enzyme upregulation. This significantly reduces the rate of cancer-initiating DNA mutations.

## 9. Chemotherapy and Radiation Protection — Supporting Treatment

ZnC has remarkable clinical evidence for protecting against treatment side effects without compromising anti-cancer efficacy.

### 9.1 Oral Mucositis Prevention

Oral mucositis affects up to 80% of patients on high-dose chemotherapy and radiation for head and neck cancers. ZnC dramatically reduces its severity:

| Clinical Setting | ZnC Intervention | Result |
|---|---|---|
| High-dose chemo before stem cell transplant | ZnC lozenges (18.75 mg, 4× daily) | Grade ≥2 mucositis: **13% vs 74% (controls)** |
| Head and neck radiation | ZnC oral solution before/during treatment | Severe mucositis reduced **3-fold**; treatment duration reduced ~40% |
| Haematological malignancies + chemo/radiation | ZnC mouth rinse | Moderate–severe mucositis: **20% vs 82% (controls)** |
| General chemotherapy | ZnC rinse | Stomatitis prevention: **68.5% success**; improvement: **84.4%** |

### 9.2 Broader Treatment Support

| Side Effect | ZnC's Protective Action |
|---|---|
| **Taste disorders** (dysgeusia) | Zinc is essential for taste bud function; ZnC helps restore taste during/after treatment |
| **Radiation oesophagitis** | ZnC reduces oesophageal inflammation during chemoradiotherapy |
| **Gut mucositis** | Mucosal protection extends throughout the GI tract |
| **General GI toxicity** | Anti-inflammatory and mucosal healing effects reduce treatment-related GI damage |

> **Key insight:** ZnC allows patients to tolerate more aggressive treatment regimens by protecting normal tissues — without interfering with the anti-cancer effects of chemo/radiation. Multiple clinical trials confirm this protective effect is real and clinically significant.

## 11. Dosage, Safety, and Bioavailability

### 11.1 Recommended Dosage

| Context | Dose |
|---|---|
| **General supplementation** | 37.5–75 mg ZnC per day (providing ~16–34 mg elemental zinc) |
| **Mucositis prevention (clinical)** | 18.75 mg ZnC lozenge × 4 daily (75 mg total ZnC) |
| **Japanese approved dose (peptic ulcer)** | 75 mg ZnC twice daily (150 mg total) |
| **Current protocol** | 15 mg elemental zinc at lunch |

### 11.2 Safety Considerations

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Upper intake (US)** | 40 mg elemental zinc/day (tolerable upper limit) |
| **Copper depletion risk** | Prolonged zinc >25 mg/day may impair copper absorption → monitor or co-supplement copper |
| **GI side effects** | At high doses: nausea, stomach pain (ZnC's mucosal protection reduces this vs other zinc forms) |
| **Drug interactions** | Zinc can reduce absorption of tetracycline/quinolone antibiotics; may interact with cisplatin |
| **Prostate cancer caution** | Very high zinc doses (>75 mg/day) or long-term use (>15 years) associated with increased prostate cancer risk in some studies |

### 11.3 Bioavailability

| Zinc Form | Bioavailability | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Zinc-L-Carnosine** | High | Chelated form with extended gut residence; sustained release to mucosa |
| Zinc gluconate | Good | Well-absorbed organic salt |
| Zinc citrate | Good | Well-absorbed |
| Zinc picolinate | Good | Good absorption |
| Zinc bisglycinate | Good | Well-tolerated |
| Zinc oxide | **Poor** | Inorganic form; poorly absorbed — avoid |

**Absorption enhancers/inhibitors:**
- **Phytates** (whole grains, legumes) significantly **reduce** zinc absorption
- **Animal protein** enhances zinc uptake
- Take zinc **2–3 hours apart** from calcium, iron supplements, and certain antibiotics

> **Protocol consideration:** Switching from generic "Zinc 15 mg" to **Zinc-L-Carnosine** would provide the same zinc but with the additional anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, mucosal-protective, and immune-modulating benefits documented above. The chelated form also has superior gut bioavailability.
