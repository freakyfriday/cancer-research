# Resistance Training — The Body's Built-In Anti-Cancer Medicine

> **Focus:** How muscle resistance training fights cancer through multiple biochemical pathways — releasing tumour-suppressive myokines, activating immune surveillance, preventing muscle wasting (cachexia), prolonging survival, and paradoxically reducing fatigue even when patients feel too exhausted to move. The muscle is not just structure — it is the body's largest endocrine organ, and resistance training turns it into a cancer-fighting factory.

---

## 1. The Fatigue Paradox — Why Training When Tired Is Critical

The single greatest barrier to resistance training for cancer patients is **cancer-related fatigue (CRF)** — an overwhelming, persistent exhaustion that doesn't improve with rest and affects up to 90% of patients during treatment. The instinct is to stop moving. This is catastrophically wrong.

### 1.1 Why Rest Makes It Worse

| What Happens with Rest | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Muscle mass declines (disuse atrophy) | Less myokine production → weakened anti-cancer signalling |
| Cardiovascular fitness drops | Reduced oxygen delivery → less efficient immune cell mobilisation |
| Mitochondrial function deteriorates | Energy production collapses → deeper fatigue |
| Inflammatory markers rise | Pro-tumour microenvironment strengthens |
| Insulin sensitivity drops | Higher circulating insulin/IGF-1 → tumour growth promotion |

> **The vicious cycle:** Fatigue → inactivity → muscle loss → worse fatigue → deeper inactivity → accelerating decline.

### 1.2 Why Even Light Resistance Training Breaks the Cycle

Exercise is now considered the **first-line treatment for cancer-related fatigue** — more effective than any medication. Even 10 minutes of structured activity outperforms pharmacological interventions for CRF.

**How it works at the cellular level:**

- **Mitochondrial biogenesis** — training stimulates the creation of new mitochondria in muscle cells via the **PGC-1α** pathway, restoring cellular energy production capacity
- **Neurotransmitter boost** — exercise increases serotonin, dopamine, and endorphin levels, directly modulating the brain's perception of fatigue
- **Improved oxygen delivery** — enhanced cardiovascular output means tissues receive more O₂ for aerobic metabolism
- **Inflammation reduction** — even a single session lowers circulating pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β), which are direct drivers of CRF
- **Functional capacity** — as muscle strength improves, daily activities require a smaller percentage of maximum output, reducing perceived effort

### 1.3 Clinical Evidence for Fatigue Reduction

| Finding | Source |
|---|---|
| 10–35 sessions of resistance training significantly reduce CRF vs standard care | Meta-analyses (Cochrane, NIH) |
| 12 weeks of resistance training during treatment measurably lessens fatigue | AAFP systematic review |
| 58% reduction in fatigue reported in breast cancer survivors with RT | SCIRP clinical data |
| Combined aerobic + resistance training is the most effective CRF strategy | Breast Cancer Research Foundation |
| A single 10-minute walk outperforms medication for acute fatigue relief | Baptist Health clinical studies |

> **Key insight:** The patient who feels too tired to train is the patient who needs it most. Cancer-related fatigue is not like healthy-person tiredness — it is driven by inflammatory cytokines, mitochondrial dysfunction, and muscle wasting. Training directly attacks each of these causes. The fatigue gets better *because* you train, not *in spite of* it.

## 2. Myokines — The Muscle's Anti-Cancer Secretome

When skeletal muscle contracts under resistance, it functions as an **endocrine organ**, releasing hundreds of signalling molecules called **myokines** into the bloodstream. These myokines communicate with virtually every organ system, including the immune system and tumour tissue directly.

### 2.1 Key Anti-Cancer Myokines Released by Resistance Training

| Myokine | Primary Anti-Cancer Mechanism | Effect on Cancer Cells |
|---|---|---|
| **Interleukin-6 (IL-6)** | Acute, pulsatile release during exercise activates AMPK in tumour cells and signals immune cells | Suppresses tumour proliferation (distinct from chronic IL-6, which is pro-tumour) |
| **Oncostatin M (OSM)** | Reduces breast cancer cell proliferation; signals through JAK/STAT pathway | Inhibits cancer cell growth, particularly breast cancer |
| **Decorin** | Tumour suppressor — binds and neutralises EGFR, Met, and IGF-IR on cancer cells | Blocks oncogenic signalling, promotes apoptosis and autophagy in cancer cells |
| **SPARC** | Linked to colon cancer tumorigenesis prevention | Inhibits colon cancer cell growth; exercise-specific SPARC release is protective |
| **Irisin** | Inhibits PI3K/Akt, activates AMPK, suppresses mTOR | Inhibits migration and metastasis in lung, bone, and pancreatic cancers |
| **IL-15** | Activates and expands NK cells and CD8+ T cells | Enhances immune surveillance against tumours |

### 2.2 The Post-Exercise Serum Effect

One of the most striking findings in exercise oncology:

1. Blood samples are drawn from cancer patients **immediately after** a resistance training session
2. This post-exercise serum is applied to cancer cells in a petri dish
3. Cancer cell growth is **inhibited by 20–30%** compared to pre-exercise serum
4. The effect is reproducible and occurs after **a single session**

This demonstrates that resistance training transforms the blood itself into an anti-cancer medium. The myokine cocktail released during training creates a systemic environment hostile to cancer growth.

### 2.3 Exercise IL-6 vs Chronic IL-6 — A Critical Distinction

| Feature | Exercise IL-6 (Acute) | Chronic IL-6 (Tumour-Derived) |
|---|---|---|
| Release pattern | Sharp spike during exercise, returns to baseline within hours | Sustained, low-grade elevation |
| Effect on AMPK | **Activates AMPK** → suppresses mTOR → anti-proliferative | Does not activate AMPK effectively |
| Effect on inflammation | **Anti-inflammatory** — triggers IL-10 release, suppresses TNF-α | **Pro-inflammatory** — drives NF-κB → tumour survival |
| Effect on cancer | Suppresses tumour growth | Promotes tumour growth, invasion, angiogenesis |

> **Key insight:** The same molecule (IL-6) has opposite effects depending on its release pattern. Resistance training produces the anti-cancer version.

## 3. Immune System Activation — Mobilising the Cancer-Killing Army

Resistance training doesn't just release myokines — it directly enhances the function and deployment of the immune cells responsible for identifying and destroying cancer.

### 3.1 Natural Killer (NK) Cell Enhancement

NK cells are the immune system's **first-line cancer killers** — they identify and destroy abnormal cells without needing prior exposure.

- Resistance training increases circulating NK cell numbers by **up to 150–300%** during and immediately after exercise
- NK cell **cytotoxic activity** (their ability to kill cancer cells) is enhanced
- Regular training leads to sustained improvements in NK cell function at rest — the immune system becomes a better cancer-surveillance machine even between sessions

### 3.2 Cytotoxic T Cell (CD8+) Activation

CD8+ cytotoxic T cells are the **adaptive immune system's precision killers** — they recognise specific cancer antigens and destroy those cells.

- Resistance training alters CD8+ T cell metabolism, making them **more energetically efficient** at killing
- Exercise promotes the redistribution of CD8+ T cells from lymphoid tissue into the bloodstream and tumour tissue
- Training reduces T cell **exhaustion markers**, keeping the immune cells functional rather than burned out
- Desirable changes in adaptive immune cell responses have been documented in cancer survivors engaging in resistance exercise

### 3.3 M1 Macrophage Polarisation

Tumour-associated macrophages (TAMs) usually adopt an **M2 phenotype** that promotes tumour growth, angiogenesis, and immune evasion. Resistance training shifts macrophage polarisation toward the **M1 phenotype**:

| Macrophage Type | Role | Exercise Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **M1 (anti-tumour)** | Pro-inflammatory, presents antigens, activates T cells, kills cancer | ↑ Promoted by resistance training |
| **M2 (pro-tumour)** | Anti-inflammatory, promotes tissue remodelling, supports tumour escape | ↓ Reduced by resistance training |

### 3.4 Kynurenine Reduction

Resistance training reduces serum **kynurenine (KYN)** levels. KYN is a tryptophan metabolite that:
- Suppresses T cell function
- Promotes tumour immune evasion via the IDO/TDO pathway
- Is elevated in many cancer patients

By lowering KYN, resistance training removes a biochemical handbrake on the immune system's ability to attack cancer.

> **Key insight:** Resistance training transforms the immune landscape from one that tolerates cancer to one that actively hunts it. It mobilises NK cells, sharpens T cells, repolarises macrophages, and removes immunosuppressive metabolites — creating a hostile environment for tumour survival.

## 4. Biochemical Pathways Activated by Resistance Training

Resistance training triggers a cascade of molecular signalling events that directly oppose the pathways cancer relies on for growth and survival.

### 4.1 AMPK Pathway — The Metabolic Tumour Suppressor

**AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK)** is the cell's master energy sensor. When energy is depleted during resistance training:

```
Resistance Training
    → ATP consumption ↑ → AMP:ATP ratio rises
        → AMPK activated
            → mTOR inhibited → protein synthesis ↓ → cancer cell growth blocked
            → Autophagy ↑ → damaged cellular components cleared
            → Fatty acid oxidation ↑ → shifts metabolism away from anabolic state
            → PGC-1α activated → mitochondrial biogenesis
            → Glucose uptake regulated
```

AMPK activation mimics the anti-cancer effects of metformin (a diabetes drug now studied for cancer prevention) — but achieved naturally through muscle contraction.

### 4.2 mTOR Inhibition in Cancer Cells

The **mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR)** is a master growth regulator that cancer cells hijack for unlimited proliferation. Resistance training myokines reduce mTOR activity in cancer cells:

- Suppresses protein synthesis needed for cancer cell division
- Induces **apoptosis** (programmed cell death) in cancer cells
- Blocks the anabolic signals cancer cells need to grow

> **Important distinction:** mTOR is *activated* in the exercising muscle (driving beneficial muscle protein synthesis and hypertrophy) while being *inhibited* in cancer cells by exercise-released myokines. The signalling is tissue-specific.

### 4.3 PI3K/Akt Pathway Inhibition

The **PI3K/Akt** pathway is one of the most frequently mutated and hyperactivated pathways in cancer. Resistance training opposes it:

- Improves **insulin sensitivity** → reduces circulating insulin and IGF-1 levels
- Lower insulin/IGF-1 → less PI3K/Akt activation in cancer cells
- Irisin released during exercise directly **inhibits PI3K/Akt signalling**
- Reduces the availability of growth factor ligands that feed this pathway

### 4.4 NF-κB Suppression — Silencing Inflammatory Survival

**NF-κB** is a transcription factor that drives inflammation-mediated cancer survival, proliferation, and treatment resistance.

- Resistance training reduces chronic activation of NF-κB signalling
- Lowers pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β) that drive NF-κB
- This reduces cancer cell survival signalling and makes tumours more vulnerable to immune attack and treatment

### 4.5 Insulin / IGF-1 Axis — Cutting the Growth Factor Supply

| Factor | Cancer-Promoting Effect | Resistance Training Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Insulin** | Activates PI3K/Akt → proliferation | Improved insulin sensitivity → lower circulating levels |
| **IGF-1** | Potent mitogen → drives cell growth | Reduced bioavailable IGF-1; increased IGFBP-3 (binding protein that sequesters IGF-1) |

Cancer cells have abundant insulin and IGF-1 receptors. Reducing circulating levels of these growth factors starves cancer cells of proliferative signals.

### 4.6 DNA Repair Enzyme Upregulation

Resistance training supports **genomic stability** by:
- Increasing expression of DNA repair enzymes (OGG1, APE1)
- Reducing oxidative DNA damage through improved antioxidant defences
- Enhancing the cell's ability to detect and correct mutations before they progress to malignancy

### 4.7 PGC-1α and Mitochondrial Biogenesis

Resistance training activates **PGC-1α** (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma coactivator 1-alpha):

- Stimulates the creation of new, functional mitochondria
- Restores oxidative phosphorylation capacity
- Improves metabolic flexibility — the body's ability to switch between fuel sources
- Enhances antioxidant enzyme expression
- Supports the energy production needed for immune cell function

> **Key insight:** Resistance training simultaneously activates AMPK (tumour-suppressive energy sensor), inhibits mTOR and PI3K/Akt in cancer cells (growth pathways), suppresses NF-κB (inflammatory survival), reduces insulin/IGF-1 (growth factors), and enhances DNA repair. These are the same pathways targeted by multiple anti-cancer drugs — achieved here through muscle contraction alone.

## 5. Preventing Cachexia — Preserving the Body's Reserves

Cancer cachexia — the involuntary loss of muscle mass and body weight — is one of the most devastating consequences of advanced cancer. It affects up to **80% of cancer patients** and is directly responsible for an estimated **20–30% of all cancer deaths**. Resistance training is the most effective countermeasure.

### 5.1 What Cachexia Does

```
Tumour
    → Releases pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6 chronic, IL-1β)
    → Activates ubiquitin-proteasome system → muscle protein breakdown ↑↑
    → Activates autophagy-lysosome pathway → cellular self-digestion
    → Suppresses mTOR → muscle protein synthesis ↓↓
    → Increases myostatin → further blocks muscle growth
    → Results in: severe muscle wasting, weakness, poor treatment tolerance, death
```

### 5.2 How Resistance Training Counters Cachexia

| Cachexia Mechanism | Resistance Training Countermeasure |
|---|---|
| **Ubiquitin-proteasome activation** (protein breakdown) | Inhibits proteasomal degradation; shifts balance toward synthesis |
| **mTOR suppression** in muscle | Activates mTOR *in muscle* (via mechanical loading) → restores protein synthesis |
| **Myostatin overexpression** | Regular loading reduces myostatin signalling |
| **Pro-inflammatory cytokines** | Anti-inflammatory myokine release opposes cytokine-driven wasting |
| **Mitochondrial dysfunction** | PGC-1α activation restores mitochondrial quality and number |
| **Oxidative stress** | Enhanced antioxidant enzyme expression reduces oxidative damage |
| **Anabolic resistance** (muscle won't respond to growth signals) | Mechanical tension from resistance training can *overcome* anabolic resistance, restoring muscle protein synthetic response |

### 5.3 Clinical Outcomes Against Cachexia

| Outcome | Evidence |
|---|---|
| 25–30% improvement in muscle strength | Breast cancer survivor RT studies |
| 10% improvement in quality of life | Systematic reviews of RT in cancer patients |
| Preservation of lean body mass during chemotherapy | Multiple RCTs |
| Improved treatment tolerance and completion rates | Oncology exercise trials |
| Reduced hospitalisations and treatment delays | Exercise oncology meta-analyses |

### 5.4 Why Muscle Mass = Survival Reserve

Muscle tissue serves as the body's **largest amino acid reservoir**. During cancer treatment:

- Chemotherapy damages rapidly dividing cells → the body needs amino acids for repair
- The immune system requires amino acids (especially glutamine, leucine) for immune cell proliferation
- Liver gluconeogenesis uses muscle-derived amino acids to maintain blood glucose
- Wound healing and tissue repair depend on adequate amino acid supply

**More muscle = more reserve = longer the body can fight.** Patients who maintain muscle mass tolerate more treatment cycles, experience fewer dose reductions, and survive longer.

> **Key insight:** Every kilogram of muscle preserved through resistance training is a reserve bank of amino acids, immune support, and metabolic capacity. Cachexia kills because it depletes these reserves. Resistance training refills them — even during active treatment.

## 6. Survival Data — The Numbers

The survival benefits of resistance training in cancer patients are now supported by large-scale clinical trials and meta-analyses.

### 6.1 All-Cause Mortality Reduction

| Population | Mortality Reduction | Study |
|---|---|---|
| Cancer patients with higher muscular strength | **31–46%** lower risk of death from any cause | Multi-cohort studies |
| Cancer patients engaging in RT ≥1x/week | **33%** reduction in all-cause mortality | NIH meta-analysis |
| Per incremental increase in muscular strength | **11%** additional reduction in mortality risk | Dose-response analysis |
| Per unit increase in cardiorespiratory fitness | **18%** additional reduction in mortality risk | Dose-response analysis |
| Advanced cancer (stage 3–4) with maintained fitness | **8–46%** reduced risk of all-cause mortality | Large cohort analysis |

### 6.2 Cancer-Specific Outcomes

| Cancer Type | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Colon cancer** | The CHALLENGE trial (international RCT): exercise group showed **37% reduction in mortality**; regular physical activity including strength training significantly prolongs life |
| **Breast cancer** | 8-year follow-up of supervised high-intensity RT: significantly lowered risk of death; RT reduces recurrence rates |
| **Lung / digestive cancers** | **19–41%** lower risk of death with maintained muscular strength |
| **All cancer types** | Muscular strength identified as a **clinical predictor of mortality** — stronger patients live longer |

### 6.3 Treatment Tolerance

Patients who engage in resistance training during treatment:
- Complete more chemotherapy cycles at full dose
- Experience fewer treatment delays
- Have lower rates of hospitalisation
- Report higher quality of life scores
- Are more likely to receive and tolerate additional lines of therapy

> **Key insight:** Resistance training doesn't just help patients feel better — it directly extends survival. The CHALLENGE trial's 37% mortality reduction in colon cancer is comparable to the benefit of many chemotherapy regimens. Muscle strength is emerging as a clinical vital sign in oncology.

## 7. Practical Application — Training Despite Fatigue

### 7.1 Minimum Effective Dose

The evidence does **not** require exhaustive gym sessions. Even modest resistance training provides significant anti-cancer benefits:

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **Frequency** | 2–3 sessions per week (even 1x/week shows benefit) |
| **Duration** | 20–30 minutes per session |
| **Intensity** | Moderate — enough to challenge the muscle, not exhaust the patient |
| **Exercises** | Major muscle groups: legs, chest, back, shoulders, core |
| **Equipment** | Bodyweight, resistance bands, light dumbbells — no gym required |
| **Progression** | Gradual — increase resistance or volume as strength improves |

### 7.2 Training on Bad Days

When fatigue is severe:

1. **Reduce volume, not frequency** — do fewer sets/exercises but still train
2. **Bodyweight counts** — sit-to-stand from a chair, wall push-ups, supported squats all qualify as resistance training
3. **10 minutes is enough** — even brief sessions trigger myokine release and immune activation
4. **Permission to be light** — a resistance band arm curl at low intensity still contracts muscle and releases anti-cancer signalling molecules
5. **Movement is medicine** — the worst workout is better than the best rest day when facing cancer

### 7.3 What to Expect

| Timeframe | Expected Response |
|---|---|
| **Single session** | Immediate myokine release; post-exercise serum shows anti-cancer activity; mood improvement |
| **2–4 weeks** | Noticeable strength gains; fatigue begins decreasing; sleep improves |
| **6–12 weeks** | Measurable fatigue reduction (up to 58%); quality of life improvement; lean mass preservation |
| **Ongoing** | Sustained immune enhancement; maintained muscle reserve; cumulative survival benefit |

## 8. Synergy with Other Protocol Elements

Resistance training amplifies the effects of other interventions in the protocol:

### 8.1 Complementary Mechanisms

| Protocol Element | Synergy with Resistance Training |
|---|---|
| **Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBOT)** | RT improves blood flow and oxygen delivery → enhances HBOT's tumour oxygenation; both activate immune cells |
| **Sauna Therapy** | Both induce heat-shock proteins (HSPs) → improved protein folding, cellular repair, and immune activation |
| **Vitamin C (IV)** | RT reduces chronic inflammation → may enhance Vitamin C's pro-oxidant selectivity for cancer cells; both improve immune function |
| **Quercetin** | Both activate AMPK and suppress mTOR; Quercetin's anti-inflammatory effects complement RT's cytokine modulation |
| **Fenbendazole** | RT's myokine-mediated disruption of tumour glucose metabolism complements Fenbendazole's HK2 suppression |
| **Green Tea (EGCG)** | Both modulate PI3K/Akt/mTOR; EGCG's antioxidant effects may protect muscle while RT enhances immune function |
| **Butyrate** | Both reduce NF-κB-driven inflammation; Butyrate supports gut-immune axis while RT enhances systemic immunity |

### 8.2 Timing Considerations

- **Train before sauna** — RT first maximises myokine release; sauna afterwards may enhance heat-shock protein response
- **Supplements around training** — most anti-cancer supplements can be taken as per normal schedule; no known contraindications with RT
- **HBOT and RT on same day** — both are safe together; enhanced oxygenation may support recovery
